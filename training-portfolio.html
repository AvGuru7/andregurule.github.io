My Philosophy on Company Training

My approach is grounded in the work of Cathy Moore (Map It: The Hands-On Guide to Strategic Training Design) and Ruth Clark (Evidence-Based Training Methods). Corporate training is less about transferring general knowledge and more about targeting the right information to drive a specific, measurable result.

It starts with clearly understanding the goal — and then honestly asking whether training is even the right solution. Sometimes the answer is a process adjustment. Sometimes it's a single well-designed job aid. Jumping straight to a course when neither is needed wastes time and erodes trust in the training function.

Adults also come to training with widely different skill levels, and effective programs account for that. Content needs to meet novices where they are without boring experienced practitioners — and those practitioners should be brought into the conversation. When skilled employees share what they know, everyone benefits, including the instructor.

The rise of AI puts corporate training — and how we work in general — at a fascinating and somewhat unsettling crossroads. I used AI to help build this website, and it is a genuinely remarkable tool. But I believe it needs to be approached with care. In the context of training, AI opens a real opportunity to cultivate new talent, if organizations are willing to invest the time to do it right. It should not replace entry-level workers. It should be used to accelerate their development — ensuring that we are building a strong, capable workforce for the future, not hollowing one out.
